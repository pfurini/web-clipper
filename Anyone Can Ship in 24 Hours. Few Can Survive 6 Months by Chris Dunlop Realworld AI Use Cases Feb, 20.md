I’m going to mock myself to start this article.

This article is indicative of AI coding. “I built something in 10 minutes.” “I built something in a day.” It’s also indicative of a lot of stories you read about and also when you are at a party and people talk about AI, it usually is a form of this.

And look, I get it. I wrote that article. I meant every word. The speed _is_ genuinely remarkable. You can sit down with Cursor or Replit or Claude and have something working before your coffee goes cold. That part is real.

The part that’s missing from every single one of these stories, mine included, is what happens on day 31. Day 90. Day 180.

As a sidenote, I think now we are safely in the “You can ignore most major new product announcements for 6 months, the good ones will still be around then phase”.

Software Is Alive and It Hates You
----------------------------------

Here’s something I didn’t understand when I moved from civil engineering to software, and it took me years to articulate properly.

A bridge is an object. Software is an organism.

Objects degrade predictably. You can model the fatigue life of a steel beam. You can calculate when concrete will need resurfacing. It’s physics. It’s maths. It’s knowable.

Software doesn’t degrade. It _mutates_. The environment around it changes constantly, APIs update, dependencies shift, user behaviour evolves, security vulnerabilities get disclosed, hosting providers adjust pricing — and your software has to keep adapting or it dies. Often this doesn’t even happen slowly, it just stops working one Tuesday afternoon and nobody knows why.

Kevin Kelly wrote in _The Inevitable_ that systems rust while software becomes. That’s poetic, and partially true. Software does become. It also _becomes incompatible_. It becomes out of date. It becomes a liability. Every piece of running software is in a constant race against the entropy of its own ecosystem.

So when someone says “I built an app in 10 minutes,” what they actually did was bring an organism into existence. That organism is now subject to every environmental pressure its ecosystem can throw at it. And they’ve given it no immune system whatsoever.

Every AI Project Is Competing With Minecraft
--------------------------------------------

And here’s the thing the ecosystem pressures aren’t just technical. The most lethal environmental pressure on software is whether you actually care enough to keep it alive. Every organism needs a host, and the host is your attention.

I built that gym app with Replit in 10 minutes. You know what else exists? A perfectly good gym app in the App Store, built by a team of people whose actual job is maintaining it. My app wasn’t competing against API changes or dependency updates. It was competing against me playing Minecraft with my brother on a Sunday evening. My brother lives overseas and this is the way that we catchup on our server that we have had for 13 years.

This is the survival pressure that nobody writes about. Software doesn’t just need code. It needs someone who gives enough of a damn to fix it when it breaks at 9pm on a Wednesday. And “I built this in 10 minutes” is almost a guarantee that the emotional investment required to sustain it doesn’t exist.

The speed that made it exciting to create is the same speed that makes it easy to abandon. You didn’t labour over it. You didn’t wrestle with it. You didn’t earn it. So when it demands your attention six weeks later, when something breaks and it needs you, you feel nothing. You just close the tab.

I believe that now the apps and website that survive aren’t the ones with just the best code, they are going to be the ones where someone has a reason to keep showing up. It’s the whole missionaries vs mercenaries thing. If I built something in 10 minutes I’m basically a mercenary by default.

The Film Set Problem
--------------------

AI coding tools produce software that looks complete the way a film set looks like a real town. Walk down the main street and it’s convincing. The storefronts have signs. The windows have curtains. From the camera angle, you’d swear people live here.

Walk around the back and there’s nothing. No plumbing. No wiring. No foundations. The buildings are facades held up by scaffolding, designed to look right from exactly one direction.

This is what most AI-generated software actually is. It handles the scenario you tested. It looks correct in the demo. It works when you use it the way you imagined someone would use it. The moment a real user does something you didn’t imagine, enters their name with an emoji, pastes a URL into a phone number field, opens it on a tablet, or as a client we had recently did, logged in on their mobile during sign up, then swapped to their computer and that created a strange loop hole.

The difference between a film set and a real town isn’t visible in photographs. You have to try to live there. The difference between a demo and a product isn’t visible in a screen recording. You have to try to depend on it.

This is why “I built this in 10 minutes” demos are so convincing and so misleading at the same time. You’re looking at the photograph. You haven’t tried to live there yet.

What Survival Actually Looks Like
---------------------------------

So what separates the things that last?

It’s tempting to say better code, better tools, better architecture. And those help. But the projects I’ve seen survive past six months share something more fundamental than any technical choice.

Someone involved understood that shipping was the beginning of the work. Not the end. Not the milestone. The starting line.

This is a cultural problem more than a technical one. The entire narrative around AI coding is oriented towards the moment of creation. The tweet. The demo. The “look what I made.” Everything after that moment, the monitoring, the bug fixes, the user support, the dependency updates, the thankless evening when you notice the database is filling up faster than expected, none of that makes for good content. None of that gets likes.

But that’s the job. That was always the job.

But I have audited myself and all the repos that I have built and of all the ideas I have had, I have used precisely 3 websites for more than 6 months.

Client Projects Are Different
-----------------------------

I should end by saying that the work we do at my agency doesn’t follow this pattern. Our client projects survive. Every single one of them is still running.

And the reason is simple: money.

When a client is paying real money for a solution, the entire dynamic changes. Their problems aren’t “I wonder if I could build a gym tracker.” Their problems are three-month problems at minimum, sometimes multi-year problems. If the solution was a day’s work, they’d have solved it with a spreadsheet already. The fact that it reached us means it’s survived a series of natural stage gates, someone identified the pain, someone approved a budget, someone picked up the phone. By the time we start building, the problem has already proven it matters enough for someone to spend money on it.

That’s the filter. That’s the immune system the weekend projects don’t have.

But really, this just confirms the central thesis of this entire article. The technology doesn’t determine whether software survives. The tools don’t determine it. The speed of creation certainly doesn’t determine it. What determines it is whether someone cares enough to see it through and keeps caring after the excitement of creation has worn off.

Money is one version of caring. It’s a forcing function. When you’re paying for something, you pay attention to it. You check in. You report bugs. You ask for improvements. You have a reason to keep showing up.

The weekend project has none of this. It has a moment of excitement and then silence.

The cool part we have now though is you can build things and then see which ones stick around. In my case it is 3 simple websites.
